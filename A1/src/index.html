Biography

Jimi Hendrix, an iconic figure in the 1960s rock music scene, mesmerized audiences with his groundbreaking electric guitar skills. Despite facing a challenging childhood, Hendrix found solace in music and became widely recognized as one of the most creative and influential musicians of the 20th century.

Born Johnny Allen Hendrix in 1942, he later adopted the name James Marshall at his father's request. Jimi discovered his deep passion for music at a young age and honed his skills by emulating artists like B.B. King, Muddy Waters, and Buddy Holly. Despite a tumultuous family life, Hendrix dedicated himself to mastering the guitar and developing a unique style.

Gifted an old ukulele and later an acoustic guitar by his father, Hendrix's musical journey began to take shape. He formed bands like The Velvetones and The Rocking Kings while still in high school, captivating audiences with his electrifying performances. At 19, he made the bold decision to drop out of school and pursue music full-time.

In 1961, Hendrix enlisted in the U.S. Army, following in his father's footsteps. Despite his military duties, he formed a band called The King Kasuals and continued to pursue music. However, a parachute jump gone wrong resulted in a career-ending injury and his honorable discharge in 1962. This marked a turning point as Hendrix embarked on a remarkable musical journey that would shape the course of rock history.

After leaving the military, Hendrix pursued a career as a session guitarist under the name Jimmy James. Working with artists such as Little Richard, B.B. King, and Sam Cooke, he showcased his exceptional talent and versatility. In 1965, he formed his own band, Jimmy James and the Blue Flames, and performed in New York City's Greenwich Village.

Hendrix's musical abilities caught the attention of Chas Chandler, the bassist of The Animals. Impressed by his talent, Chandler invited Hendrix to London to form a new band. Hendrix embraced the opportunity and formed the Jimi Hendrix Experience with Noel Redding on bass and Mitch Mitchell on drums. The Experience quickly gained popularity with their electrifying performances and Hendrix's virtuosic guitar playing.

In June 1967, Hendrix's performance at the Monterey International Pop Festival propelled him to global stardom. His unforgettable rendition of "Wild Thing," complete with guitar theatrics and setting his instrument on fire, became an iconic moment in music history. The Jimi Hendrix Experience became one of the most popular and highest-grossing touring acts in the world.

Hendrix continued to push the boundaries of music with albums like "Are You Experienced", "Axis: Bold As Love", and "Electric Ladyland." His innovative fusion of blues, psychedelic rock, funk, and jazz, coupled with his virtuosic guitar skills and inventive use of effects, created a sonic landscape previously unheard of.

Tragically, Hendrix's extraordinary journey was cut short in 1970 due to complications related to drug use. His untimely death left a void in the music world, but his legacy lives on. Jimi Hendrix's music continues to inspire and captivate audiences, and his innovative guitar techniques and genre-defying sound have influenced generations of musicians.

From his humble beginnings as a self-taught guitarist to becoming a global music icon, Hendrix's legacy is a testament to the power of passion and creativity. He revolutionized the way the electric guitar is played and opened doors for countless artists to explore new sonic possibilities. Jimi Hendrix will forever be remembered as a true pioneer and visionary, whose music transcends time and continues to ignite the imagination of music lovers worldwide.

Video

Jimi Hendrix (1973) from Robert Alan (Scoobzy) McDowell on Vimeo.

This documentary was made three years after Jimi Hendrix's untimely death. At the time it was an example of how a visual biography should be done, but some of the information in it needs revising in the light of new information uncovered over the years. The film contains concert footage spanning the Marquee in 1967 to his last UK performance at the third Isle of Wight festival in 1970; along the way we see classic performances at Monterey (1967), Woodstock (1969), Fillmore East (1969/70), and Berkeley (1970). A double album was released to tie-in with the film, containing the complete performances in the film, along with interviews with people in the film (not necessarily the same interviews). The film is worth seeing for Jimi's performances, and to hear what his contemporaries have to say about him (Eric Clapton, Mitch Mitchell, Lou Reed, Mick Jagger, Pete Townsend, and others).

- Robert Alan (Scoobzy) McDowell
